SLIDE 1 - Title                                                    (~20 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Good morning. I'm Wenxuan Xu. This is my final-presentation talk for
CSEE E6868: Intra-Layer Parallelism on Tiled NVDLA Accelerators in ESP."


SLIDE 2 - Background                                               (~45 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"The setup is simple. Edge deep-learning workloads grow faster than what
one small accelerator can handle. Tiled architectures - where you replicate
smaller accelerators and let them cooperate - scale better than monolithic
designs. ESP already integrates NVDLA as a third-party accelerator, but
only one tile at a time on a single workload. The goal of this project is
to enable coarse-grained INTRA-LAYER PARALLELISM: take a single layer of
a neural network and split its computation across multiple NVDLA tiles."


SLIDE 3 - NVDLA Overview                                           (~45 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Quick orientation on NVDLA. On the left you see how ESP integrates a
third-party accelerator: it goes into a tile shell that connects to the
rest of the SoC through the NoC. On the right is NVDLA's own software
stack - a user-mode driver and a kernel-mode driver between the compiled
loadable file and the hardware. Both of those layers matter for what I'll
describe next, because we ended up working at the compiler layer rather
than touching the runtime."


SLIDE 4 - Why Output-Channel Split?                                (~50 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"There are four natural ways to partition a convolution layer across
tiles. We chose OUTPUT-CHANNEL SPLIT as the primary strategy for three
reasons. First, no halo or border issues - each tile reads the SAME input,
so there's no inter-tile synchronization on activation regions. Second,
each tile gets an independent subset of weights and biases - no
cross-tile reduction needed for partial sums. Third, the correctness
check is trivial: concatenate the per-tile outputs along the channel
axis and compare bit-exactly to the unsplit baseline. That made it the
lowest-risk path to a working proof of concept."


SLIDE 5 - Approach Pivot Since Midterm                             (~50 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"The proposal originally said we'd modify NVDLA's runtime and driver to
split work at execution time. Looking at the actual runtime code at
midterm, I realized the runtime is good at re-binding buffers and
selecting a tile, but it isn't designed to reshape compiled descriptors -
that's a much bigger surgery. So I pivoted to a cleaner approach: GENERATE
THE SPLIT LOADABLES AT COMPILE TIME, and let the runtime just orchestrate
parallel launches. The compiler emits self-consistent descriptors per
partition, the runtime synchronizes one launch per tile, and validation
reduces to concat-then-compare against the full baseline. This is what we
actually built."


SLIDE 6 - System Setup & Workflow                                  (~75 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"The setup, then. On the left is the ESP SoC: four NVDLA tiles, one
shared memory tile with the LLC and DDR controller, an Ariane CPU, one
IO tile, and two empty tile slots - all on a 3x3 mesh with a 6-plane
NoC. All tiles share the 78 MHz base clock; only the DDR controller runs
in its own faster domain. The NVDLA configuration is nv_small_fpga:
8x8 INT8 MACs and 4-beat x 64-bit AXI bursts - that 32-byte burst will
come back later. Target model is ResNet-18 - 18 weighted layers, 11
distinct conv shapes that span from input-heavy to weight-heavy regimes.
The bottom bar shows the workflow: take a Caffe network, run our
split-loadable generator, stage to the FPGA's sysroot, run all 4 splits
in parallel on 4 NVDLA tiles, capture ESP hardware counters, validate
bit-exactly. End to end, fully scripted."


SLIDE 7 - Target Model: ResNet-18                                  (~60 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Quick look at the target. ResNet-18 starts with a 7x7 stride-2 stem
convolution, a maxpool, then four residual stages - layer1 through
layer4 - each containing two BasicBlocks. The first BasicBlock of
layers 2, 3, 4 downsamples spatially and doubles the channel count.
Finally average-pooling and a fully-connected layer for classification.
The detail on the left shows what a BasicBlock is: two 3x3 convolutions
in series, with a residual skip connection that adds the input back at
the end. We chose ResNet-18 because it's well-studied, INT8-friendly,
and its 11 distinct conv shapes span a wide range of arithmetic
intensities - from conv1, which is input-bound, to layer4 with 512
input x 512 output channels, which is weight-bound. That range is what
lets us see how different layers behave under the same parallelism
strategy."


SLIDE 8 - Correctness Verification - PASS = 11 / 11                (~50 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Before any performance claim, correctness. For each of the 11 distinct
conv shapes, we ran the baseline full-layer loadable on one NVDLA tile,
ran four split loadables in parallel on four tiles, concatenated the
split outputs along the channel axis, and md5-compared against the
baseline. ALL 11 LAYERS PASS BIT-EXACTLY. Output element counts match
the expected ResNet-18 shapes - conv1 producing 802,816 values, layer1
200,704, and so on. So everything downstream operates on known-correct
compute. The output-channel split methodology generalizes to any depth
and any model - what we have here is a complete workflow that handles
ResNet-18 at the per-layer level."


SLIDE 9 - Headline Result - NEGATIVE Speedup                       (~80 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Now the surprise. Comparing single-tile baseline runs against the
maximum-of-four-tiles 4-way split, conv1 goes from 22.95 million NVDLA
cycles to 75.4 million. layer4_3x3 goes from 9.7 million to 31.1
million. PER-TILE WORK RUNS ABOUT 3.2x LONGER under 4-way parallelism,
not 4x faster. The real speedup at the hardware-cycle level is roughly
0.30x - not a speedup, a SLOWDOWN. Submit-time wall-clock looks the
same: about 4x slower. And importantly, the memory-bound percentage is
essentially 100% in every measurement - acc_tot and acc_mem differ by
single-digit cycles. So the tile is in its memory-state every cycle.
The rest of this talk is figuring out WHY that is."


SLIDE 10 - Roofline Characterization                               (~90 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"To diagnose, I ran a 27-config roofline sweep, spanning arithmetic
intensities from 49 to 664 operations per byte. Three ceilings on this
plot. The horizontal black line is the COMPUTE PEAK - 128 ops per NVDLA
cycle, set by nv_small's 64 INT8 MACs. The dashed gray line is the
NoC BANDWIDTH PEAK - 8 bytes per cycle times the arithmetic intensity.
They cross at AI equals 16. The red dotted line is the PER-TILE
ACHIEVABLE ceiling - 0.49 bytes per cycle times AI. That number comes
from the burst-size and serialization analysis on the next slide.

Every one of our 27 configs has arithmetic intensity above 16 - so by
classical roofline analysis, they should all be compute-bound. They are
not. The best workload reaches only 26 ops per cycle - about 20% of
compute peak. Most points cluster near the red dotted line. THE SYSTEM
IS BANDWIDTH-BOUND, but at a very low per-tile bandwidth ceiling - far
below the NoC's theoretical capability."


SLIDE 11 - vary_h Sweep - Latency-Bound Evidence                   (~55 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Direct evidence for the latency-bound story. I held everything constant
except the spatial size H, varying it from 7 to 112. The blue line is
DRAM bytes per cycle, the orange is achieved ops per cycle. Both grow
monotonically with spatial size. Why? Bigger spatial dimensions mean
bigger sequential output write bursts, which the DMA can pipeline.
Per-request round-trip latency gets amortized across more bytes per
request. This is the textbook signature of a latency-bound system:
throughput grows when you give the DMA larger contiguous chunks to
process. IT'S NOT RAW BANDWIDTH THAT'S MISSING - it's the ability to
amortize each round-trip."


SLIDE 12 - Multi-Tile Contention - 4 Independent Copies            (~65 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Now the contention test. To isolate whether the 4-way slowdown comes
from output-channel split specifically or from something more general,
I ran four INDEPENDENT copies of the same loadable in parallel - no
cooperation, no shared work. Three configurations: one write-pipelined,
two read-latency-stalled. All three show THE SAME 3.5-to-3.9x per-tile
slowdown. And the aggregate DRAM throughput across the SoC barely
scales - 1.04 to 1.14 times single-tile, far from the ideal 4x. The
slowdown isn't a property of output-channel split. IT'S A PROPERTY OF
RUNNING 4 TILES IN PARALLEL against a shared memory subsystem."


SLIDE 13 - Root Cause - Two Stacked Mechanisms       (~110 sec) <-- KEY SLIDE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Here's the mechanism, in two parts.

Mechanism 1: BURST SIZE. The ESP NoC can carry up to 256 beats x 8 bytes
per AXI transaction - that's 2048 bytes maximum per request. NVDLA's
nv_small_fpga configuration issues only 4-beat x 8-byte bursts - just
32 BYTES per AXI transaction. That's a 64x efficiency loss on
per-transaction overhead.

Mechanism 2: OUTSTANDING-TRANSACTION SERIALIZATION. ESP allows only ONE
outstanding AXI transaction per master at a time. NVDLA can issue
multiple requests internally, but the SoC stack serializes them - no
pipelining of round-trips.

Combine the two. Each transaction takes about 65 cycles round-trip -
NoC traversal, LLC lookup, DRAM access, response. With single-
outstanding-txn rule, the next request can only fire after this one
returns. So effective per-tile bandwidth is 32 BYTES PER 65 CYCLES,
about 0.5 bytes per NVDLA cycle, about 38 megabytes per second per
tile. That's exactly the red dotted line on the roofline, and exactly
where our points cluster.

Under 4-way parallelism, four tiles compete for that one serialization
slot at the shared memory tile. Per-tile round-trip becomes 4 x 65
cycles because each tile waits through three other tiles' transactions
before its own gets served. Same per-tile bandwidth divided by N.
Aggregate throughput stays constant. THAT'S THE 0.30x SPEEDUP."


SLIDE 14 - Submit Time - Mostly Host-Side Software                 (~50 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"One more correction the audience should know about. The host-side
wall-clock submit time is much larger than the actual NVDLA hardware
time. For conv1_full, submit time is 5.79 seconds - but acc_tot at the
78 MHz tile clock is only 0.29 seconds of HW work. The rest is the
Ariane CPU running NVDLA's C++ runtime: parsing the loadable,
allocating DMA buffers, the memcpy of weights into them, and
programming hundreds of NVDLA registers over the slow MMIO path.
Important methodological point: HARDWARE-COUNTER ANALYSIS - acc_tot,
ddr_words - is the right basis for any performance claim, NOT
wall-clock."


SLIDE 15 - Milestones - Reached & Not Reached                      (~70 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Self-evaluation. On the left, what's complete: the full ResNet-18
parallelism workflow - compile, split, deploy, run on FPGA, validate;
all 11 distinct conv shapes covered; bit-exact correctness verified
per layer; the per-layer characterization; the 27-config roofline; the
multi-tile contention analysis; and the two stacked root causes
identified.

On the right, what's not reached. End-to-end ResNet-18 inference WITH
SPLIT-LAYER CHAINING - this would require automatic activation
reconstruction at every split-to-merge boundary, which is engineering
glue, not new research; the per-layer workflow is in place. Alternative
split methods - input-channel, batch, spatial - weren't pursued because
output-channel was the diagnostic priority. We found a negative result
and EXPLAINED THE CAUSE instead of positive speedup. We couldn't
directly measure NVDLA-tile NoC traffic because of a counter wiring
gap. And the 2-tile contention sweep + NVDLA-internal HW perf register
read had to be cut because we lost FPGA access before the final
session."


SLIDE 16 - Future Work - Three Concrete Experiments                (~60 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Three concrete experiments tied to the findings. First, the cheap one
- no resynthesis needed: a 2-tile and 3-tile contention sweep plus
reading NVDLA's internal CDMA stall counters via /dev/mem. This would
localize whether the bottleneck is at the per-tile AXI or the shared
memory tile. Second, a single resynth jump: rebuild with
nv_small_256_full, which adds a second NVDLA memory port. Same burst
size, but two AXI ports per tile - tests whether NVDLA's per-tile
bandwidth is the binder. Third, the bigger experiment: rebuild with
nv_medium_512, which doubles the AXI data width to 128 bits. That
directly tests the burst-size hypothesis. Other directions: more
memory tiles, input-channel split, automatic chained inference."


SLIDE 17 - Conclusions                                             (~75 sec)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Engineering: built and verified output-channel split for 11 ResNet-18
layers - bit-exact correct. Negative result: 4-way split gives about
0.30x per-tile speedup, universal across layer types and across
independent-copy workloads. Diagnosis: every workload is 100%
memory-bound in the FSM-counter sense, but the binding constraint is
PER-AXI-TRANSACTION ROUND-TRIP LATENCY, not raw DRAM bandwidth - DRAM
and NoC both have spare capacity. Two concrete structural causes:
32-byte AXI bursts versus 2048-byte NoC capacity, and ESP's single-
outstanding-transaction serialization. Under multi-tile parallelism,
those two mechanisms multiply: per-tile round-trip grows linearly with
tile count because the shared memory tile is the serialization slot.

THE BOTTOM LINE: THE NEGATIVE SPEEDUP IS A PROPERTY OF THE PLATFORM
CONFIGURATION, NOT A FLAW IN THE PARTITIONING STRATEGY. And we've
identified the exact knobs - burst length, outstanding-transaction
depth, memory-tile count - that would unlock parallel speedup.

Thank you. Happy to take questions."

================================================================================
Q&A PREP - TOP 5 LIKELY QUESTIONS
================================================================================

1.  "Did you verify the burst-size hypothesis with re-synthesis?"
    --> No, FPGA access was disconnected before final synthesis. That's the
        top of the future-work list.

2.  "How do you know the bottleneck is at the memory tile and not elsewhere?"
    --> I don't, fully. The 4-indep contention data narrows it; the 2-tile
        sweep in future-work would localize it precisely.

3.  "Why didn't input-channel split work better - it would reduce input
     duplication?"
    --> Input-channel split needs partial-sum reduction across tiles, which
        requires either a hardware combiner or an extra synchronization
        step. It's harder, and we'd still hit the shared-memory-tile
        bottleneck under contention.

4.  "Is the negative speedup specific to nv_small_fpga?"
    --> Partly. The 32B burst is. But the single-outstanding-txn rule
        applies to all ESP third-party accelerators, so this generalizes.

5.  "Could you parallelize across the layers of ResNet instead of across
     the channels?"
    --> Yes, pipeline-parallel inference where different tiles execute
        different layers - that's a separate axis the SoC could support,
        and an interesting follow-on.
